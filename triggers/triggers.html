Introduction
  • This is a book about adult behavioral change
  • A trigger is any stimulus that reshapes our thoughts and actions.
  • The attitude of embracing regret: The pain that comes with regret should be mandatory, not something to be shooed away. That pain can be motivating and triggering.

Part One — Why Don’t We Become the Person We Want to Be?
Chapter 1 — The Immutable Truths of Behavioral Change
  • Truth #1: Meaningful behavioral change is very hard to do
    • Change is hard to initiate and even harder to make it stick
    • We must appreciate inertia’s power over us
    • We do not know the mechanics of executing a change. You don’t only need motivation, you need understanding and ability for change.
  • Truth #2: No one can make us change unless we truly want to
    • Some people say they want to change, but they don’t really mean it
    • Oftentimes, you do not only need to buy into the change yourself, you have to get some buy-in from people directly associated with the change. This is why relying on other people, or having other people be part of your change increases the difficulty of change exponentially.

Chapter 2 — Belief Triggers That Stop Behavioral Change in Its Tracks
  • It is much easier, and more fun, to attack the strategy of the person who’s trying to help than to try to solve the problem
  • Excuses (variations of “The dog ate my homework”)
  • If I understand, I will do
    • This book offers common sense advice. However this advice works, it is on you to execute on it. It doesn’t sort of work or kind of work.
  • I have willpower and won’t give in to temptation
    • People who need help and structure are “weak”
    • We overestimate our willpower
    • We underestimate the power of triggers in our environment to lead us astray
  • Today is a special day
    • Use a special day as an excuse not to diet, say
    • We yield to impulse and short-term gratification because today is the Super Bowl
    • Excusing our momentary lapses as an outlier event triggered a self-indulgent inconsistency — which is fatal for change
  • At least I’m better than… 
    • We award ourself a free pass because we’re not the worst in the world
  • I shouldn’t need help and structure
    • A natural response that combines three competing impulses
      • our contempt for simplicity (only complexity is worthy of our attention)
      • our contempt for instruction and follow-up
      • our faith that we can succeed by ourselves
    • When we presume that we are better than people who need structure and guidance, we lack one of the most crucial ingredients for change: humility
  • I won’t get tired an my enthusiasm will not fade
    • When we plan to achieve our goals, we believe that out energy will not flag and that we will never lose our enthusiasm for the process of change
    • We don’t recognize self-control as a limited resource
    • The effort of sticking with the plan triggers depletion
  • I have all the time in the world
    • Two opposing beliefs that we simultaneously hold in our minds:
      • we chronically underestimate the time it takes to get anything done
      • we believe that time is open-ended and sufficiently spacious for us to get to all our self-improvement goals eventually.
    • The faith in time’s infinite patience triggers procrastination
  • I won’t get distracted and nothing unexpected will occur
    • When we make plans for the future, we seldom plan on distractions
    • Always expect a crisis will happen that will change your schedule or priorities. These events are very likely.
  • An epiphany will suddenly change my life
    • Hopes and prayers over structure 
  • My change will be permanent and I will never have to worry again
    • The Great Western Disease: “I’ll be happy when…"
    • Our belief that happiness is a static and finite goal
    • In consumer society this brainwash in inevitable
    • False sense of permanence
  • My elimination of old problems will not bring on new problems
    • As we usher an old problem out the door, a new problem usually enters.
    • The euphoria of achieving their dream job of CEO vanishes by the second meeting with the board of directors.
    • Lottery winners as an example
  • My effort will be fairly rewarded
    • Our dashed expectations trigger resentment to our change
    • Pursue change because you believe it is the right thing to do, not because of the expected benefits.
    • Pursuing change for external rewards is bad because
      • There is no certainty that you will get what you want
      • If the reward is the only motivator people revert to their old ways
    • Getting better is its own reward. This way we will never feel cheated.
  • No one is paying attention to me
    • We believe we can occasionally lapse back into bad behavior because people aren’t paying close attention.
    • This avoidance through escape through being noticed triggers a preference for isolation.
    • Note that as we revert to our previous behavior, people always notice.
  • If I change I am “inauthentic”
    • This triggers stubbornness.
    • We can change both our behavior and how we define ourselves.
  • I have the wisdom to assess my own behavior
    • We tend to credit ourselves for our victories and blame our situation or other people for our losses

Chapter 3 — It’s the Environment
  • It is hard to understand how big of an impact our environment has on us
  • We think we control our environment, but it controls us.
  • Think of the environment around you as a person. Give it a name! This personification will help you better appreciate the effect it has on you.
  • Our environment is a nonstop triggering mechanism whose impact on our behavior is too significant to be ignored.
  • One tweak in the environment can change everything.
  • The most pernicious environments are the ones that compel us to compromise our sense of right and wrong (e.g., the ultra competitive environment of the workplace)
  • Some environments are intentionally designed to lure us into acting against our interest (e.g., designing an online or store shopping experience to make a customer stay/spend!)
  • Sleep deprivation that you experience is most likely shaped by your environment. Namely, we need to consciously get into an environment where we can sleep. Due to inertia, we don’t do this and stay in whichever environment we are currently in until we cannot stay awake any longer.
  • The environment is a hyper-active shape-shifter with mutating who-when-where-and-why specifics. Every time one of these characteristics change, we are putting our goals and plans at risk.
  • Letterman experimented with room temperature to see which temperature makes the room laugh more. He discovered that a chilly 55 degrees make the audience more alert.

Chapter 4 — Identifying our triggers
  • Feedback — both the act of giving it and taking it — is our first step in becoming smarter, more mindful about the connection between our environment and our behavior.
  • Feedback teaches us to see our environment as a triggering mechanism.
  • Radar speed displays on roads (The things above speed limit signs that report back your speed in live time) work because they harness a well-established concept in behavior theory called a feedback loop.
  • Four stages of a feedback loop:
    • Evidence
    • Relevance
    • Consequence
    • Action
  • Feedback loops help us establish that our good behavior is not random. It’s logical and follows a pattern. It makes sense. It’s within our control. It’s something we can repeat.
  • As a trigger, our environment has the potential to resemble a feedback loop. It is constantly providing new information that has meaning and consequence for us and alters our behavior.
  • What if we could control our environment so it triggered our most desired behavior — like an elegantly designed feedback loop? 
    • Instead of blocking us from our goals, this environment propels us. 
    • Instead if shutting down who we are, it opens up.
  • Trigger — Any stimulus that impacts our behavior

How Do Triggers Influence Our Behavior?
  1. A behavioral trigger can be direct of indirect.
    1. Direct triggers — immediately and obviously impact behavior
    2. Indirect triggers — take a more circuitous route before influencing behavior
  2. A trigger can be internal or external
    1. External triggers — come from the environment
    2. Internal triggers — come from thoughts or feelings that are not connected with any outside stimulus. Many people meditate to dampen the internal trigger they refer to as an inner voice.
  3. A trigger can be conscious or unconscious
    1. Conscious triggers — require awareness (e.g. touching a hot stove)
    2. Unconscious triggers — shape your behavior beyond your awareness
  4. A trigger can be anticipated or unexpected
  5. A trigger can be encouraging or discouraging
  6. A trigger can be productive or counterproductive
    1. This is the most important distinction
    2. Our response to the triggers (not the triggers themselves) is what matters
    3. Highlight the distinction between what we want and what we need
  • Steps to setting up appropriate triggers:
    • Pick a behavioral goal you’re pursuing
    • List the people and situations that influence the quality of your performance
    • Chart the triggers to see if you are on the right side
  • If you don’t have a lot of stuff towards the right, and you do have a lot of stuff towards the left, you are getting more of what you want and less of what you need.
  • This exercise should
    • make us smarter about specific triggers
    • help up connect them directly to our behavioral successes and failures
  • This matrix explains why people never lose weight — not enough stuff on the right side of the matrix.

Chapter 5 — How Triggers Work
  • How does a trigger actually work within us? Are there moving parts between the trigger and the behavior.
  • ABC:
    • Antecedent — event that prompts the behavior
    • Behavior — the behavior elicited by the antecedent
    • Consequence — the consequence of the behavior
  • Habits are formed through ABC. E.g. smoking:
    • Antecedent — stress
    • Routine — nicotine stimulus
    • Reward — temporary psychic well-being
  • When people gain wait when they stop smoking, they substitute food for smoking as their routine, while the antecedent and reward remain the same.
  • Golden Rule of Habit Changing — Keep the cue and reward, but substitute the behavior with a harmless activity.
  • This works for personal behaviors, but doesn’t work for interpersonal behavior.
  • It’s the little moments that trigger some of our most outsized and unproductive responses: The slow line at the coffee shop, the second cousin who asks why you’re still single, the neighbor who doesn’t pick up after his dog, the colleague who doesn’t remove his sunglasses indoors to talk to you.
  • Will you give in to the perfectly natural impulse to express your scorn, or will you take a breath and make a smarter choice?

Chapter 6 — We Are Superior Planners and Inferior Doers
  • Hersey and Blanchard believed that leaders should:
    • keep track of the shifting levels of readiness among their followers
    • stay highly attuned to each situation
    • acknowledge that situations change constantly
    • fine-tune their leadership style to fit the follower’s readiness.
  • Further, they dissected the relationship between leaders and their followers into four distinct styles:
    • Directing. For employees requiring a lot of specific guidance to complete the task. A step by step description of a procedure. Primarily a one-way conversation with little input from the employee
    • Coaching is for employees who need more than average guidance to complete the task, but with above-average amounts of two-way dialog. Coaching is for people who want and need to learn.
    • Supporting is for employees with the skills to complete the task but who may lack the confidence to do it on their own.
    • Delegating is for employees who score high on motivation, ability, and confidence.
  • The four styles are exempt from qualitative judgment.

Measure Your Need, Choose Your Style
  • How to apply this to becoming who you want to be?
  • Within each of us, associated with each plan we have, there is one side that is the leader/planner/manager who plans to change his or her ways and there is the follower/doer/employee who must execute the plan.
  • Whether you are leading other people or leading the follower in you, the obstacles to achieving your goals are the same.
  • So, make your management style fit into your employees!
  • We often make plans that are wholly contradicted by our previous actions
  • The planner who makes a deadline is also a doer that forgets that he has never made a deadline in his life.
  • If you go into a meeting and need to be reminded of how you should behave, write that on a flashcard that you take with you.

Chapter 7 — Forecasting the Environment
  • Forecasting is what we must do after acknowledging the environment’s power over us.
  • Three interconnected stages of forecasting:
    • anticipation
    • avoidance
    • adjustment
Anticipation
  • When our performance has clear and immediate consequences, we rise to the occasion. We create our environment. We don’t let it recreate us.
Avoidance
  • Quite often our smartest response to an environment is avoiding it.
  • We often stick to enjoyable environments due to inertia, even if that is not what we should be doing at the moment.
  • Due to our delusional belief that we control our environment, we choose to flirt with temptation that comes along with it rather than walk away.
  • This is true in leadership too: Leaders sometimes need to avoid the temptation to exercise power when they would be better off showing restraint.
  • Avoid situations that tempt you to do things you don’t want to do.

Adjustment
  • Avoidance may be impossible

Chapter 8 — The Wheel of Change
  • We willfully ignore how profoundly the environment influences our behavior
  • Four options in pursuing any behavioral change: change or keep the positive elements, change or keep the negative.
    • Creating represents the positive elements that we want to create in our future.
    • Preserving represents the positive elements that we want to keep in the future.
    • Eliminating represents the negative elements that we want to eliminate in the future.
    • Accepting represents the negative elements that we need to accept in the future.

Creating
  • Creating is the glamorous poster child of behavioral change.
  • Creating is not easy for anyone.
  • If we’re satisfied with our life — not necessarily happy or delighted that we’ve exceeded our wildest expectations, just satisfied — we yield to inertia.

Preserving
  • It requires soul-searching to figure out what serves us well, and discipline to refrain from abandoning it for something new and shiny and not necessarily better.
  • Ask “What in my life is worth keeping?”

Eliminating
  • When the consequence is extreme distress, we binge on elimination.
  • The real test is sacrificing something we enjoy doing.
  • Ask “What should I eliminate?”

Accepting
  •  Business people, reluctant to admit any defeat, can’t help equating “acceptance” with “acquiescence”.
  • Our episodes of nonacceptance trigger more bad behavior than the fallout from our creating, preserving, and eliminating combined.

  • Executing on change we hold in our head is a process that requires vigilance and diligent self-monitoring. It requires devotion to rote repetition that we might initially dismiss as simplistic and undignified, even beneath us.

Chapter 9 — The Power of Active Questions
  • Active questions are the alternative to passive questions: There’s a difference between “Do you have clear goals?” and “Did you do your best to set clear goals for yourself?”
    • Did I do my best to set clear goals today?
    • Did I do my best to make progress toward my goals today?
    • Did I do my best to find meaning today? Viktor Frankl’s Man’s Search for Meaning
    • Did I do my best to be happy today?
    • Did I do my best to build positive relationships today?
    • Did I do my best to be fully engaged today?
  • The nightly specter of honestly answering these questions kept me focused on my goal of being a happier and healthier individual.
  • Active questions themselves don’t merely elicit an answer, they create a different level of engagement with your goals.
  • Your Daily Questions should reflect your objectives. You are not making it to impress anyone.
  • If you are like most people, your objectives fall into a predictable set of broad categories: health, family, relationships, money, enlightenment, and discipline.
  • Within two weeks, half of th people who try Daily Questions give up on them.
  • It is incredibly difficult for any of us to look in the mirror every day and face the reality that we didn’t even try to do what we claimed was most important in our lives.
  • That is the secret power of daily self-questioning: If we fall short on our goals eventually we either abandon the questions or push ourself into action.
  • You have to follow up with yourself if you want to change; daily questions are a good platform for this.
  • Proactive questions (asking “Did I do my best to…” instead of “Did I do…”) focus on effort instead of results, which is especially valuable when our goal is not even on the horizon.
  • As the time between planning and doing increases—and our environment intrudes with all its temptations and distractions—our enthusiasm and discipline fade.
  • Tune your objectives and reevaluate them as you go, changing them when needed.

Daily Questions Reinforce Our Commitment
  • The questions announce our intention to do something and, at risk of private disappointment or public humiliation, they commit us to doing it.
  • Asking a friend for help commits us to change, so is setting an alarm clock in the morning.

Daily Questions Ignite Our Motivation Where We Need It, Not Where We Don’t
  • Intrinsic motivation is wanting to do something for its own sake, because we enjoy it.
  • Extrinsic motivation is doing something for external rewards such as other people’s approval or to avoid punishment. Our extrinsic motivation gets shaky only when we achieve these targets—and wonder why they haven’t provided the meaning, purpose, and happiness we were hoping for.
  • Daily Questions focus us on where we need help, not where we’re doing just fine. We all have tasks and behaviors that come naturally to us, where we don’t need a boost from an outside agency. Daily Questions press us to face the hard problems in our lives, admit them, and write them down.

Daily Questions Highlight the Difference Between Self-Discipline and Self-Control
  • Behavioral change demands self-discipline and self-control.
  • Self-discipline refers to achieving desirable behavior
  • Self-control refers to avoiding undesirable behavior
  • Most people are better at one than the other.
  • These two questions attack the two different aspect separately yet achieve the same goal:
    • “Did I do my best to limit my sugar consumption?” (self-discipline)
    • “Did I do my best to say no to sweets?” (self-control)
  • Depending on who we are, that subtle adjustment can make all the difference

Daily Questions Shrink Our Goals into Manageable Increments
  • Daily Questions neutralize the archenemy of behavioral change: our impatience.
  • By focusing on effort, they distract us from our obsession with results (because that’s not what we are measuring)
  • Daily Questions remind us that:
    • Change doesn’t happen overnight
    • Success is the sum of small efforts repeated day in and day out.
    • If we make the effort, we will get better. If we don’t, we won’t.

Chapter 12 — Planner, Doer, and Coach
  • We must communicate our scores to someone somehow every day (phone contact, email, or a voice message). That someone is the coach.
  • For some people the coach is little more than a scorekeeper—someone we report to each evening without having to endure any judgment or interference. For others he is a referee. For other the coach is a full-blown adviser.
  • At the most basic level, a coach is a follow-up mechanism.
  • On a more sophisticated level, a coach instills accountability.
  • At the highest level, a coach is a source of meditation, bridging the gap between the visionary Planner and short-sighted Doer in us.
  • A coach reminds the fragile Doer what he’s supposed to do.
  • One reason we resist coaching: Our privacy — we believe some pieces of us are not to be shared with the world. We prefer to keep some of our behavioral deficits to ourselves rather than hang them out in public like laundry.
  • Another reason for keeping issues personal is that we know we need to change. We are in denial.

Chapter 13 — AIWATT
  • The first principle for becoming the person you want to be: A question you should be asking yourself whenever you must choose to either engage or “let it go”:
    • Am I willing, at this time, to make the investment required to make a positive difference on this topic?

It’s Always an Empty Boat
  • The Parable of the Empty Boat (zen):
    • The colleague that interrupts you during meetings. He thinks he’s smarter than everyone, not just you. Empty boat.
    • The aggressive driver who tailgated you for miles does that every day on any road. Empty boat.
    • The bank officer who turned down your small business loan application because of a typo on the form, she sees a form, not you. Empty boat.
  • Getting mad at people for who they are is as absurd as getting mad at a chair for being a chair.

Creating False Positives
  • Peter Drucker: “Our mission in life should be to make a positive difference, not to prove how smart or right we are.”
  • False positive: Making statements to upgrade ourselves, often at the expense of others—there are many forms of this:
    • Pedantry. A subordinate makes a grammatical error in a presentation and you correct him. Smart perhaps, but hardly a contribution that improves the room’s vibe.
    • I told you so.
    • Moral superiority.
    • Complaining.
  • From wake-up to bedtime, when we’re in contact with another human being, we face the option of being helpful, hurtful, or neutral.
  • If we don’t pay attention, we often choose to be hurtful, largely to prove we’re smarter, better, or more right than the “other guy”
  • AIWATT is the delaying mechanism we should be deploying in the interval between trigger and behavior—after a trigger creates an impulse and before behavior we may regret.
  • AIWATT creates a split-second delay in our prideful, cynical, judgmental, argumentative, and selfish responses to our triggering environment. The delay let’s us produce a more positive response:
    • Am I willing implies that we are exercising volition—taking responsibility—rater than surfing along the waves of inertia that otherwise rule our day. We are asking “Do I really want to do this?”
    • At this time reminds us that we’re operating in the present. Circumstances will differ later on, demanding a different response. The only issue is what we are facing now.
    • To make the investment required reminds us that responding to others is work, an expenditure of time, energy, and opportunity. And, like any investment, our resources are finite. We are asking “Is this really the best use of my time?”
    • To make a positive difference places the emphasis on the kinder, gentler side of our nature. It’s a reminder that we can help create a better us or a better world. If we’re not accomplishing one or the other, why are we getting involved?
    • On this topic focuses us on the matter at hand. We can’t solve every problem. The time we spend on topics where we can’t make a positive difference is stolen from topics where we can.
  • AIWAATT matters in the seemingly small moments that can shape our reputation and make or break our relationships:
    • When we confuse disclosure with honesty. 
      • We all have the good sense to shade our opinions and tamp down our needless disclosure in the inconsequential moments when doing so costs us nothing.
      • But that laudable instinct to protect others weakens when it comes up against our need to assert and protect ourselves. In those moments we employ honesty as a weapon rather than as a positive contribution to the situation.
      • Honesty is stating enough truth to satisfy the other person’s need to know
      • Too much disclosure has a more ambitious reach—often to a point where the other person suffers and feels ashamed.
    • When we have an opinion.
    • When our facts collide with other people’s beliefs. Charging at people with facts in order to change their opinions usually backfires and creates and image of you as disagreeable. 
    • When decisions don’t go our way.
      • Every decision in the world is made by the person who has the power to make the decision. Make peace with that.
    • When we regret our own decisions. 
      • When we regret our own decisions—and do nothing about it—we are no better than a whining employee complaining about his superiors. We are yelling at an empty boat, except it’s our boat.
  • AIWATT is a reminder that our environment tempts us many times a day to engage in pointless skirmishes and we can do something about it—by doing nothing.

Part Three — More Structure, Please

Chapter 14—We Do Not Get Better Without Structure
  • The Business Plan Review (BPR) process is the most effective use of organizational structure that I have ever observed.
  • Many people are too proud to admit they need structure. This is a mistake.
  • They consider repetitious activity as mundane, uncreative, somehow beneath them.
  • Structure limits our options so that we’re not thrown off course by externalities. Imposing structure on parts of our day is how we seize of our otherwise unruly environment.
  • For the simple tasks of interacting with other people we prefer to wing it—for reasons that sound like misguided variations on “I shouldn’t need to do that”.
  • We tell ourselves: “I’m a confident, successful adult. I shouldn’t have to constantly monitor if I’m being nice or if people like me.”
    • Or we hold ourselves blameless for any interpersonal friction; it’s always someone else’s fault, not ours.

Chapter 15—But It Has to Be the Right Structure
  • The structure has to fit the situation and the personalities involved.

Chapter 16—Behaving Under the Influence of Depletion
  • Why do our discipline and decisiveness fade at the end of the day, to the point where we opt to do nothing instead of doing something enjoyable or useful? Ego depletion
  • We possess a limited conceptual resource called ego strength, which is depleted through the day by our various efforts at self-regulation—resisting temptations, making trade-offs, inhibiting our desires, controlling our thoughts and statements, adhering to other people’s rules.
  • The more decisions we’re obliged to make, the more fatigued we get in handling subsequent decisions. This is called decision fatigue, a state that leaves us with two courses of action
    • We make careless choices
    • We surrender to the status quo and do nothing.
  • Unlike being physically tired, however, we’re usually unaware of depletion, so we don’t appreciate how it is grinding us down, affecting our behavioral discipline—and exposing us to bad judgment and undesirable actions.
  • Under depletion’s influence we are more prone to inappropriate social interactions, such as talking too much, sharing intimate personal information, and being arrogant, we are less likely to follow social norms, we are less helpful.
  • We need to make a conscious effort to understand our level of depletion in any given moment.
  • When other people call us out for disappointing or upsetting them, we need to apologize by saying “I had a rough day”, or “I’m exhausted”.
  • Making big decisions late in the day is an obvious risk.
  • If we provide ourselves with enough structure, we don’t need discipline.
  • Structure can be a surrogate for self-discipline. It allows us to surrender to the routine without needing to resort to self discipline.

Chapter 17—We Need Help When We’re Least Likely to Get It
  • What about all the unguarded interpersonal moments that aren’t marked down on our schedules?
    • The annoying colleague, noisy neighbor, rude customer, angry client, distressed child, disappointed spouse who unexpectedly demands attention when we’re neither prepared nor in the best shape to respond well?
  • We lack structural tools to handle bewildering interpersonal challenges.

The Awful Meeting
  • Many common interpersonal challenges where we respond poorly without structure.
  • We need a simple structure that
    • anticipates that our environment will take a shot at us
    • triggers a smart, productive response rather than foolish behavior
  • Suggestion: Pretend you will be tested on the following questions prior to entering any meeting or engagement that you feel you will not enjoy:
    • Did I do my best to be happy?
    • Did I do my best to find meaning?
    • Did I do my best to build positive relationships?
    • Did I do my best to be fully engaged?
  • This is the structure!

Chapter 18—Hourly Questions 
  • In any situation we can live in one of three dimensions: past, present, or future
  • When we commit to being miserable in a dull meeting, we’re doing one of two things, neither of them good:
    • We’re wallowing in the past, remembering with regret and frustration all the previous boring meetings we’ve attended
    • We’re thinking about the future, muddling through the meeting with impatience or misguided longing for whatever’s next.
  • When we know we’ll be tested—even if it’s just pretend—we’re forcing ourselves to live in the present. We’re alert, aware, and mindful of our behavior and everyone else’s, because we sense that in the very immediate future we will have to account for our actions.
  • Adapting Daily Questions into Hourly Questions creates a powerful structure for locating ourselves in the moment.
  • When we decide to behave well and our first steps are successful, we often achieve a self-fulfillment  momentum where we don;t have to try as hard to be good. Good behavior becomes the sunk cost we hate to sacrifice.
  • Also, Hourly Questions are great because the simpler the structure the more likely we will stick to it.
  • Hourly Questions help with:
    • Pre-awareness. Successful people are generally good at anticipating environments where their best behavior is at risk. They are rarely ambushed by a tough negotiation, awful meeting, challenging confrontation. They know what they are getting into before they walk into the room.
    • Commitment. Successful people are not wishy-washy about a course of action. Choosing Hourly Questions as a structure and articulating the specific questions is a commitment device—certainly better than hoping things work out.
    • Awareness.We’re most vulnerable to our environments whims when we ignore its impact on us. We don’t have time to forget our situation or get distracted from our objective—because the next test is coming in sixty minutes.
    • Scoring. Grading our performance adds reflection to mindfulness. We are more self-conscious when we’re being observed and judged
    • Repetition.
  • Hourly Questions, in contrast to Daily Questions, are for the short game—when we require a burst of discipline to restrain our behavioral impulses for a defined period of tine.

Chapter 19 — The Trouble with Good Enough 
  • We can never achieve perfect patience or generosity or empathy or humility.
  • Good enough isn’t necessarily a bad thing. In many areas of life, chasing perfection is a fool’s errand, or at least a poor use of our time.
  • Let’s look at four environments that trigger good enough behavior.
    • When our motivation is marginal.
      • When our lack of skill at any task dramatically reduces our motivation to do that task, defaulting to some form of good enough is a shrewd option. It’s not ideal, but it is better than kidding ourselves.
      • Marginal motivation produces marginal outcome.
      • We fail at New Year’s resolutions because our goals are almost always about marginal stuff, which we pursue with marginal motivation.
      • Finally, we don’t appreciate how quickly our motivation can turn marginal at the first signs of progress (once we are good enough).
    • When we are working pro bono.
      • When we find ourselves in a position where we have an excuse to do less than our best, how many of us grab the pro bono excuse as a lifeline?
      • Integrity is an all-or-nothing virtue. We need to display it in whatever obligations we’ve made. But we sometimes think more about our situation than the people counting on us.
      • Takeaway: If you think doing folks a favor justifies doing less than your best, you’re not doing anyone any favors, including yourself. People forget your promise and remember your performance.
    • When we behave like “amateurs”.
      • Who among us hasn’t noticed how in our home environment we behave in ways we’d never tolerate in a work environment (because work is a “professional setting”)?
      • If you consider yourself an amateur in a field, you will program yourself for mediocrity in that field.
      • Takeaway: Being good over here does not excuse being not so good over there.
    • When we have compliance issues.
      • People have compliance issues for two reasons:
        • They think they have a better way of doing something (classic need-to-win syndrome)
        • They’re unwilling to commit fully when it means obeying someone else’s rules of behavior (classic not-invented-here syndrome)
      • We all resist being told how to behave, even when it’s for our own good.
      • Most of us don’t notice our episodes of noncompliance, although we quickly spot them in others. It’s the other guy who breaks a confidence, or litters, or texts while driving. Not us. We would never do that.
      • Takeaway: When we engage in noncompliance, we’re not just being sloppy and lazy. It’s more aggressive and rude than that. We’re thumbing our noses at the world, announcing: “The rules don’t apply to us. Don’t rely on us. We don’t care.”

Chapter 20—Becoming the Trigger
  • Change begins with a commitment to improve and notifying people of your plan.
  • The goal is that we begin to change our environment rather than be changed by it. The people around us sense this. We have become the trigger.

Part Four—No Regrets
Chapter 21—The Circle of Engagement
  • The most important change is of the type that has to do with the quality of your interaction with others. The most important change is behavioral change.
  • We waste time on issues we don’t feel that strongly about. We wish instead of do.
  • We limit ourselves with rigid binary thinking. Any environment is supple and offers more than either/or.
  • We suffer a failure of imagination. Some types of change never even enter our minds.
  • Forcing people to think about their environment in the context of fundamental desires like happiness, purpose, and engagement concentrates the mind, makes people reflect on how they’re measuring up in those areas—and why.
  • We rarely blame ourselves for mistakes or bad choices when the environment is such a convenient fall guy.
  • When we lack awareness (in many cases because we are lost in what we’re doing or feeling), we are easily triggered. The gap from trigger to impulse to behavior is instantaneous.
  • Awareness makes a difference!

Chapter 22—The Hazard of Leading a Changeless Life
  • There is one aspect of our lives where we wear changelessness as a badge of honor—our interpersonal behavior and our resistance to changing how we treat other people.
    • The sister we haven’t seen or spoken to in years because of some long-forgotten grievance.
    • The old friend we still tease with a cruel childhood nickname he’s long outgrown.
    • The neighbor we’ve seen for years and, out of syncs or inertia or indifference, have never introduced ourselves to.
    • The customers we resent for the demands they place on us.
    • The scolding response when a child disappoints us.
  • When we prolong negative behavior—both the kind that hurts the people we love or the kind that hurts us in some way—we are leading a changeless life in the most hazardous manner. We are willfully choosing to be miserable and making others miserable, too. The time we are miserable is time we can never get back.
