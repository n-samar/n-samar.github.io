Sommerville: Chapter 10 — Sociotechnical systems
  • A system that includes people, software, and hardware.
  • Systems perspective on security and dependability
  • Systems tend to have properties that only become apparent when their components are integrated and operate together.
  • Software systems are not isolated systems but rather essential components of more extensive systems that have some human, social, or organizational purpose.
  • Layers of a sociotechnical systems stack:
    • The equipment layer. Composed of hardware devices, some of which may be computers.
    • The operating system layer. This layer interacts with the hardware and provides common facilities for higher software layers.
    • The communications and data management layer. Middleware.
    • The application layer. 
    • The business process layer. How does the business and its people interact with the SW?
    • The organizational layer. Higher-level strategic processes as well as business rules, policies, and norms that should be followed when using the system.
    • The social layer. At this layer, the laws and regulations of society that govern the operation of the system are defined.
  • Thinking holistically about systems, rather than simply considering software in isolation, is essential when considering software security and dependability. 
  • How can you assess risks of software failure if you do not think about affects it will have on layers above it? I.e. system failures (rather than software failures) are the key problem.
  • Examine how the software interacts with its immediate environment to ensure that:
    • Software failures are contained within enclosing layers of the system stack and do not seriously affect the operation of adjoining layers. Particularly, software failures should not lead to system failures
    • You understand how faults and failures of non-software layers of the system stack may affect the software. You may decide to build checks into the software to help detect these failures, and how to recover from them.
  • Many so-called software failures are not a consequence of inherent software problems but rather are the result of trying to change the software to accommodate modified system engineering requirements.

Complex systems
  • A system is a purposeful collection of interrelated components, of different kinds which work together to achieve some objective.
  • Systems that include software fall into two categories:
    • Technical computer-based systems. These are systems that include hardware and software components but not procedures and processes. Individuals and organizations use technical systems for a particular purpose but knowledge of this purpose is not part of the system.
    • Sociotechnical systems. Have defined operational processes that people are inherent parts of the system. May be affected by external constraints such as national laws and regulatory policies.
  • When developing sociotechnical systems, you need to understand the environment in which they are used.
  • Organizational factors from the system’s environment that may affect requirements, design, and operation include:
    • Process changes. The system may impose new process rules on employees.
    • Job changes. New systems may de-skill the users in an environment or cause them to change the way they work.
    • Organizational changes. The system may change the political power structure within an organization.
  • Factors important to security and dependability:
    • They have emergent properties that are properties of the system as a whole, rather than associated with individual parts of the system.
    • They are often nondeterministic.
    • The extent to which the system supports organizational objectives does not just depend on the system itself. It also depends on the stability of these objectives, the relationships, and conflicts between organizational objectives and how people in the organization interpret these objectives.

Emergent system properties
  • Two types of emergent properties:
    • Functional emergent properties when the purpose of a system only emerges after its components are integrated.
    • Non-functional emergent properties, which relate to the behavior of the system in its operational environment. This includes reliability, performance, safety, and security.
  • Three perspectives of sociotechnical system reliability:
    • Hardware reliability. 
    • Software reliability. 
    • Operator reliability. How likely is it that the operator of a system will make an error and provide incorrect input?

  • Behavior of people is inherently nondeterministic, making reliability hard to test.

Systems engineering
  • Involves all activities including procuring, specifying, designing, implementing, validating, deploying, operating, and maintaining sociotechnical systems.
  • Three overlapping stages in the lifetime of large and complex sociotechnical systems:
    • Procurement or acquisition. 
      • Decide on purpose of the system.
      • High level system requirements defined
      • Decisions o fidstribution of functionality across HW, SW, and people. 
      • Purchase of required components.
    • Development.
    • Operation. 
      • System is deployed
      • Users are trained
  • System engineering involves involvement of range of disciplines.
  • Possible issues with integrating interdisciplinary systems:
    • Different disciplines use the same words to mean different things.
    • Each discipline makes assumptions about what can or can’t be done by other disciplines.
    • Disciplines try to protect their professional boundaries and may argue for certain design choices that favor the employment of their expertise.

System procurement
  • Involves decisions concerning scope, budget, timescales, high-level system requirements, required purchases.
  • The drivers of procurement decisions are:
    • The state of other organizational systems. If the organization has a mixture of systems that cannot easily communicate or that are expensive to maintain, then procuring a replacement system may lead to significant business benefits.
    • The need to comply with external regulations. That is, replacement of noncompliant systems.
    • External competition. 
    • Business reorganization. Reorganizations lead to changes in business processes that require new systems support.
    • Available budget. Budget is usually the main limit to scope.
  • Possible issues with integrating off-the-shelf components:
    • Off-the-shelf components do not usually match requirements exactly, unless requirements have been written with these components in mind. This means you might have to modify requirements.
    • After a contract has been selected, there is a contract negotiation period where you may have to negotiate further changes to the requirements.
  • Configuration errors, where entry points to the system are not properly secured, are a major source of security problems.
  • Outsourcing development or using off-the-shelf systems gives you little control over system security.

System development
  • Requirements are a bridge between the procurement and the development processes.
  • Six essential activities in systems development:
    • Requirements development. 
    • System design. 
    • Subsystem engineering. This stage involves developing the software components of the system; configuring off-the-shelf hardware and software.
    • System integration. Components are put together to create a new system. Emergent properties start to become apparent.
    • System testing. 
    • System deployment. Process of making the system available to its users.
  • Incremental integration is usually superior to integrating all components at the same time:
    • The subsystems will never be developed at the same time.
    • Incremental integration reduces the cost of error location.

System operation
  • Operational processes are the processes that are involved in using the system for its defined purpose.
  • Operational processes should be designed to be flexible and adaptable, as anticipating all use cases is virtually impossible.
    • There should be no constraints on the order of operations, and the system should not rely on particular processes being followed.

Human error
  • Two ways of viewing human error:
    • The person approach. Errors are considered to be the responsibility of the individual.
    • The systems approach. The basic assumption is that people will inevitably make mistakes. Good systems should recognize this fact and work to support error detection and correction.
  • Reducing system failure rate due to human error:
    • Include different types of barriers.
    • Minimize the number of latent conditions in the system.

System evolution
  • System evolution involves reentering the development process to make changes and extensions to the system’s hardware, software and operational processes.
  • Inherently costly because:
    • Proposed changes have to be analyzed carefully from a  business and technical perspective.
    • Changes to one subsystem may adversely affect the performance of behavior of other subsystems.
    • The reasons for original design decisions are often unrecorded.
    • System that has been changed a lot is harder and harder to change further.
  • Changes to a system are often a source of problems and vulnerabilities.
