Sommerville: Chapter 26 — Process Improvement
  • Two different approaches to process improvement and change:
    • The process maturity approach, which has focused on improving process and project management and introducing good software engineering practice into an organization. The primary goals of this approach are improved product quality and process predictability
    • The agile approach, which has focused on iterative development and the reduction of overheads in the software process.
  • W. E. Deming’s idea of statistical quality and process control: Measure the number of product defects and relate the defects to issues with processes.
  • Agile emphasizes the importance of good people over process quality to software product quality.

The Process Improvement Process
  • You must always consider the local environment and culture and how this may be affected by process change proposals, prior to accepting new or foregoing old processes.
  • It is not possible to make process improvements that optimize all process attributes simultaneously.
  • Process improvement is cyclical:
    • Process measurement. Attributes of the current project or the product are measured.
    • Process analysis. Process weaknesses and bottlenecks are identified.
    • Process change. Proposed to address some of the identified process weaknesses.

  • Relevant process characteristics:
    • Understandability. To what extent is the process explicitly defined and how easy is it to understand the process definition?
    • Standardization. To what extent is the process based on a standard generic process? This may be important to customers that require conformance with a set of defined process standards. To what extent is the same process used in all parts of the company?
    • Visibility. Do the process activities culminate in clear results, so that the progress of the process is externally visible?
    • Measurability. Does the process include data collection or other activities that allow process or product characteristics to be measured?
    • Supportability. Is the defined process acceptable to and usable by the engineers responsible for producing the software product?
    • Acceptability. Is the defined process acceptable to and usable by the engineers responsible for producing the software product?
    • Reliability. Is the process designed in such a way that process errors are avoided or trapped before they result in product errors?
    • Robustness. Can the process continue in spite of unexpected problems?
    • Maintainability. Can the process evolve to reflect changing organizational requirements or identified process improvements?
    • Rapidity. How fast can the process of delivering a system from a given specification be complete?

  • Without significant and concrete data on a process or the software developed using that process, it is impossible to assess the value of process improvement.
  • Process improvement is a long-term, continuous activity.

Process Measurement
  • Essential for process improvement.
  • Three types of process metrics can be collected:
    • The time taken for a particular process to be completed. 
    • The resources required for a particular process.
    • The number of occurrences of a particular event. This may include the number of defects discovered during code inspection, the number of requirements changes requested and the average number of lines of code modified in response to a requirements change.
  • Fundamental issue: How to know what information about the process should be collected to support process improvement? Goal-Question-Metric paradigm
  • Goal-Question-Metric paradigm answers three critical questions:
    • Why are we introducing new process improvements?
    • What information do we need to help identify and assess improvements?
    • What process and product measurements are required to provide this information?
  • GQM Abstractions:
    • Goals. A goal is something that the organization is trying to achieve. It should not be directly concerned with process attributes but rather with how the process affects products or the organization itself (e.g. improved level of process maturity, shorter product development time or increased product reliability).
    • Questions. These are refinements of goals where specific areas of uncertainty related to the goals are identified. Normally, a goal will have a number of associated questions that need to be answered. E.g. for shortening development times, the associated question may be “Where are the bottlenecks in our current process?”, “How can the time required to finalize product requirements with customers be reduced?”, “How many of our tests are effective in discovering product defects?”
    • Metrics. These are the measurements that need to be collected to help answer the questions and to confirm whether or not process improvements have achieved the desired goal.
  • The advantage of GQM is that it separates organizational concerns (the goals) from specific process concerns (the questions).
  • When you observe changes to a metric, it is always tempting to attribute these changes to the process changes that you have introduced. However, it is dangerous to make simplistic assumptions about improvements. Changes in a metric can be caused by something completely different.

Process analysis
  • Process analysis is the study of processes to help understand their key characteristics and how such processes are performed in practice by the people involved.
  • Process analysis has some closely related objectives:
    • To understand the activities involved in the process and the relationships between these activities.
    • To understand the relationships between the process activities and the measurements that have been made.
    • To relate the specific process or processes that you are analyzing to comparable processes elsewhere in the organization, or to idealized processes of the same type.
  • The most commonly used techniques of process analysis are:
    • Questionnaires and interviews. Engineers and managers are questioned about what actually goes on with a project. Interviews are more open-ended than questionnaires; you start with a prepared script of questions but adapt these according to the responses that you get from different people.
    • Ethnographic studies. Here, participants are observed as they work, may be used to understand the nature of software development as a human activity. More likely than interviews to discover the true process used.

  • When analyzing a process, it is often useful to start with a process model that defines the activities in the process and the inputs and outputs of these activities.
  • Examples of questions about the formal process model:
    • What activities take place in practice but are not shown in the model?
    • Are there process activities, shown in the model, that you (the process actor) think are inefficient? In what ways are they inefficient and how might these be improved? How do these inefficient activities affect process measurements that may have been made?
    • What happens when things go wrong? Does the team continue to follow the process defined in the model, or is the process abandoned and emergency action taken?
    • Who are the actors involved at different stages in the process and how do they communicate? What bottlenecks commonly occur in the information exchange?
    • What tool support is used for the activities shown in the model? Is this effective and universally used? How could tool support be improved?
  • An exception will affect and usually alter, in some way, the resources, budgets, or schedules of a project. It is difficult to predict them all in advance. However, the standard should be adapted to cope with these issues.

Process Change
  • Involves making modifications to the existing process.
  • Process changes should be driven by specific improvement goals
  • Five key stages in the process change process:
    • Improvement identification. Involves using the results of the process analysis to identify ways to tackle quality problems, schedule bottlenecks, or cost inefficiencies identified.
    • Improvement prioritization. This stage is concerned with assessing possible changes to the process, and prioritizing them for implementation.
    • Process change introduction. Implies putting new procedures, methods and tools into place and integrating them with other process activities. You must allow enough time to introduce changes and ensure that these are compatible with other process activities and organizational procedures and standards.
    • Process training. Without training, it is not possible to gain the full benefits of process changes.
    • Change tuning. You need a tuning phase where minot problems can be discovered, and modifications to the process can be proposed and introduced.
  • It is generally unwise to introduce too many changes at the same time.
  • Difficulties involved in change processes:
    • Resistance to change. 
    • Change persistence. It is common for process innovations to be discarded after a short time and for the processes to revert to their previous state.
  • As a manager, you have to be sensitive to the feelings of the people affected when introducing process change.
  • Be realistic of short-term benefits of change. The benefits of process change are long-term rather than short-term

The CMMI process improvement framework
  • Software Capability Maturity Model (CMM)
  • CMMI - integrated Capability Model
  • Intended as framework for process improvement that has broad applicability across a range of companies.
  • Allows an organization’s system development and management processes to be assess and assigned a maturity level from 1 to 5.
  • The principal model components are:
    • A set of process areas that are related to software process activities. 
    • A number of goals, which are abstract descriptions of a desirable state that should be attained by an organization. In CMMI, goals are associated with each process area and define the desirable state for that area.
    • A set of good practices, which are descriptions of ways of achieving a goal. Several specific and generic practices may be associated with each goal within a process area.
  • Generic goals and practices are not technical but are associated with the institutionalization of good practice.
  • A CMMI assessment involves examining the processes in an organization and rating these processes or process areas in a six-point scale that relates to the level of maturity in each process area. These are the six grade levels:
    • 0 - Incomplete. At least one of the specific goals associated with the process ares is not satisfied.
    • 1 - Performed. The goals associated with the process area are satisfied, and for all processes the scope of the work to be performed is explicitly set out and communicated to the team members.
    • 2 - Managed. At this level, the goals associated with the process area are met and organizational policies are in place that define when each process should be used. There must be documented project plans that define the project goals. Resource management and process monitoring procedures must be in place across the institution.
    • 3 - Defined. This level focuses on organizational standardization and deployment of processes. Each project has a managed process that is adapted to the project requirements from  a defined set of organizational processes. Process assets and measurements are collected and used for future process improvement.
    • 4 - Quantitatively managed. At this level, there is an organizational responsibility to use statistical and other quantitative methods to control sub-processes. That is, collected process and product measurements must be used in process management.
    • 5 - Optimizing. At this level, the organization must use the process and product measurement to drive process improvement. Trends must be analyzed and the processes adapted to changing business needs.

The staged CMMI model
  • The key difference between the staged and the continuous CMMI models is that the staged model is used to assess the capability of the organization as a whole, whereas the continuous model measures the maturity of specific process areas within the organization.
  • The staged CMMI helps organizations implement specific levels to advance their CMMI level.
  • How to make the project planning process a managed process?
    • Establish and maintain an organizational policy for planning and performing the project planning process.
    • Provide adequate resources for performing the project management process, developing the work products and providing the services of the process.
    • Monitor and control the project planning process against the plan and take appropriate corrective action.
    • Review the activities, status and results of the project planning process with high-level management, and resolve any issues.

  • Major disadvantage: the prescriptive nature the staged model. It assumes all goals and practices at one level are implemented before the transition to the next level.

The continuous CMMI model
  • Continuous maturity models do not classify an organization according to discrete levels.
  • Advantage is that companies can pick and choose processes for improvement according to their own needs and requirements.
