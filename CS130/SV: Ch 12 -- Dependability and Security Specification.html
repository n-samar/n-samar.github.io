Sommerville: Chapter 12 — Dependability and security specification
  • Topic: How to specify functional and non-functional requirements?
  • Software system can work, but the system as a whole can still fail.

Risk-driven requirements specification
  • The steps involved:
    • Risk identification. 
    • Risk analysis and classification. Select risks that are serious and/or plausible and analyze them further. 
    • Risk decomposition. Each risk is analyzed to discover potential root causes of that risk.
    • Risk reduction. 
  • Possible phases of risk analysis:
    • Preliminary risk analysis, where major risks from the system’s environment are identified. Goal is to develop an initial set of security and dependability requirements.
    • Life-cycle risk analysis. Mostly concerned with risks that arise from system design decisions. Since this is done post-design, you can add risks that are typically associated with your choice of technology and system architecture.
    • Operational risk analysis, which is concerned with the system user interface and risks from operator errors.

Safety specification
  • Safety critical systems are systems in which failures may affect the environment of the system and cause injury or death to the people in that environment.
  • The steps involved:
    • Risk identification. In safety specification, this is the hazard identification process that identifies hazards that may threaten the system.
    • Risk analysis. The process of hazard assessment to decide which hazards are the most dangerous and/or the most likely to occur.
    • Risk decomposition. Discover events that can lead to the occurrence of a hazard.
    • Risk reduction.
  • Three classifications of risk:
    • Acceptable risk
    • Risk tolerated only if risk reduction is impractical or excessively expensive
    • Unacceptable region — risk cannot be tolerated.
  • To do a fault tree analysis, you start with the hazards that have been identified. For each hazard, you then work backwards to discover the possible causes of that hazard. You put the hazard at the root of the tree and identify the system states that can lead to that hazard.
  • Strategies for risk reduction:
    • Hazard avoidance.
    • Hazard detection and removal.
    • Damage limitation.

Reliability specification
  • Two types of reliability requirements:
    • Non-functional. Define the number of failures that are acceptable during normal use of the system.
    • Functional. Define system and software functions that avoid, detect, or tolerate faults in the software and so ensure that these faults do not lead to system failure.
  • Reliability metrics:
    • Probability of failure on demand. Should be used in situations where a failure on demand can lead to a serious system failure.
    • Rate of occurrence of failures.  Good metric for when demands to a system are made regularly
    • Mean Time to Failure. Use if absolute time between failures in important.
    • Availability. The availability of a system reflects its ability to deliver services when requested.
  • You can use two types of time to measure reliability and availability:
    • Calendar time.
    • Processor time. Use this when there is a lot of down time of the system (telephone-switching network).

Non-functional reliability requirements
  • Advantages in deriving quantitative reliability specifications:
    • It helps stakeholders understand that there are different types of system failure, and it makes clear to them how expensive it is to achieve high levels of reliability.
    • It provides a basis for assessing when to stop testing a system.
    • It can be used as a tool for assessing design strategies intended to improve the reliability of the system.
    • If a regulator needs to certify the system, evidence of meeting reliability targets is important.
  • Note that it is hard for system stakeholders to translate their practical experience into quantitative specifications.
  • A fundamental problem with over specification is that it may be practically impossible to show that a very high level of reliability or availability has been achieved.
  • Organizations must be realistic about whether it is worth specifying and validating a very high level of reliability.
  • How to avoid over specification of system reliability (i.e. too high of a reliability)?
    • Specify the availability and reliability requirements for different types of failures.
    • Specify the availability and reliability requirements for different services separately.
    • Decide whether you really need high reliability in a software system or whether the overall system dependability goals can be achieved in other ways. For example, error detection and correction might be a better solution.

Functional reliability specification
  • Involves identifying requirements that define constraints and features that contribute to system reliability.
  • Three types of functional reliability requirements:
    • Checking requirements. Involve detecting incorrect or out-of-range inputs before they are processed by the system.
    • Recovery requirements. Usually involve maintaining copies of the system and its data and specifying how to restore system services after a failure.
    • Redundancy requirements. Specify redundant features of the system that ensure that a single component failure does not lead to a complete loss of service.

Security specification
  • Security is a more challenging problem than safety because:
    • The environment in safety concerns is usually not hostile.
    • When system failures occur that pose a risk to safety, you look for the errors or omissions that have caused the failure. However, attackers may actively try to conceal their means of attack.
    • Usually acceptable to shut down or degrade system service to avoid safety failure. However, attackers may attack the system with the goal of degrading performance.
    • Safety-related events are not generated by an intelligent adversary.
  • 10 types of security requirements:
    • Identification requirements specify if a system should identify its users before interacting with them.
    • Authentication requirements specify how users are identified.
    • Authorization requirements specify the privileges and access permission of identified users.
    • Immunity requirements specify how a system should protect itself against viruses, worms, and similar threats.
    • Integrity requirements specify how data corruption should be avoided.
    • Intrusion detection requirements specify what mechanisms should be used to detect attacks on the system.
    • Non-repudiation requirements specify that a party in a transaction cannot deny its involvement in the transaction.
    • Privacy requirements specify how data privacy is to be maintained.
    • Security auditing requirements specify how system use can be audited and checked.
    • System maintenance security requirements specify how an application can prevent authorized changes from accidentally defeating its security mechanisms.

  • The steps in the risk-driven security requirements process:
    • Asset identification (risk identification), where system assets that may require protection are identified.
    • Asset value assessment (risk analysis), where you estimate the value of the identified assets.
    • Exposure assessment (risk analysis), where you assess the potential losses associated with each asset. Involves direct losses, such as the theft of information, the cost of recovery, and the possible loss of reputation.
    • Threat identification (risk analysis), where you identify the threats to system assets.
    • Attack assessment (risk decomposition), where you decompose each threat into attacks that might be made on the system and the possible ways in which these attacks may occur.
    • Control identification (risk reduction), where you propose the controls that might be put in place to protect an asset (e.g. encryption).
    • Feasibility assessment (risk reduction), where you assess the technical feasibility and the costs of the proposed controls. Don’t use expensive controls to protect low value assets.
    • Security requirements definition (risk reduction), where knowledge of the exposure, threats, and control assessments is used to derive system security requirements.

Formal specification
  • The advantages of developing a formal specification and using this in a formal development process are:
    • You develop a deep and detailed understanding of the system requirements while developing a formal specification.
    • The specification can be automatically analyzed to discover inconsistencies and incompleteness.
    • You can automatically transform the specification to executable code.
    • Program testing costs are reduced, since the model is verified by a formal specification.
  • Arguments against using formal system specifications:
    • Problem owners and domain experts cannot understand a formal specification.
    • It is hard to estimate the advantage of using a formal specification.
    • Most software engineers do not know how to write formal specifications.
    • Hard to scale current approaches to formal specification
    • Not compatible with agile development methods.
