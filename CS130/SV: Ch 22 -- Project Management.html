Sommerville: Chapter 22 — Project Management
  • Two key project management activities:
    • People management
    • Risk management
  • The software manager’s job is to ensure that the software project meets and overcomes these constraints as well as delivering high-quality software.
  • Important project management goals:
    • Deliver the software to the customer at the agreed time
    • Keep overall costs within budget
    • Deliver software that meets the customer’s expectations
    • Maintain a happy and well-functioning development team
  • What makes project management particularly challenging for software projects?
    • The product is intangible. 
    • Large software projects are often ‘one-off’ projects. Even managers who have a large body of previous experience may find it difficult to anticipate problems, since each project is fairly unique.
    • Software processes are variable and organization-specific. 
  • Responsibilities of project managers:
    • Project planning. This includes planning, estimating, and scheduling project development, and assigning people to tasks, as well as monitoring progress.
    • Reporting. Project managers are usually responsible for reporting on the progress of a project to customers and to the managers of the company developing the software. They have to communicate at a range of levels, from detailed technical information to management summaries.
    • Risk management. Project managers have to assess the risks that may affect a project, monitor these risks, and take action when problems arise.
    • People management.
    • Proposal writing. The first stage in a software project may involve writing a proposal to win a contract to carry out an item of work. These typically involve cost and schedule estimates and justifies why the organization would be best to implement the solution.

Risk Management
  • Three related categories of risk:
    • Project risk. Risks that affect the project schedule or resources. One example of project risk is losing an experienced designer.
    • Product risk. Risks that affect the quality of performance of the software being developed. An example of a product risk is the failure of a purchased component to perform as expected.
    • Business risks. Risks that affect the organization developing or procuring the software. For example, a competitor introducing a new product.
  • You should record the results of the risk analysis in the project plan along with a consequence analysis, which sets out the consequences of the risk for the project, product, and business.
  • Outline of risk management processes:
    • Risk identification.
    • Risk analysis. Asses the likelihood and consequences of each risk
    • Risk planning. Make plans to address the risk, either by avoiding it or minimizing its effects.
    • Risk monitoring. You should regularly assess the risk and your plans for risk mitigation and revise these when you learn more about the risk.
  • Risk identification checklist:
    • Technology risks. Risks that derive from the software or hardware technologies that are used to develop the system.
    • People risks. Risks that are associated with the people in the development team.
    • Organizational risks. 
    • Tools risks. 
    • Requirements risks. Risks that derive from changes to the customer requirements and the process of managing the requirements change.
    • Estimation risks. Risks that derive from the management estimates of the resources required to build the system.
  • Risk analysis:
    • Discretize risk probability and consequences into buckets to make thinking about the easier.
  • Not all risks need be monitored, but how many should be depends on the project.
  • During risk planning you assign a coping strategy to each of the key risks listed previously.
  • Three catogires of risk management strategies:
    • Avoidance strategies. Minimize probability of risk materializing.
    • Minimization strategies. Minimize effect if risk materializes.
    • Contingency plans. Following these strategies means that you are prepared for th worst and have a strategy in place to deal with it.
  • At every management review, you should consider and discuss each of the key risks separately.

Managing People
  • As a project manager, make a commitment to bettering your soft skills.
  • Four critical factors in people management:
    • Consistency. People in a project team should all be treated in a comparable way.
    • Respect. Different people have different skills and managers should respect these differences.
    • Inclusion. People contribute effectively when they feel that others listen to them and take account of their proposals. It is important for all views to be considered.
    • Honesty. As a manager, you should always be honest about what is going well and what is going badly in the team. You should also be honest about your level of technical knowledge.
  • People management is something that has to be based on experience, rather than learned from a book.
  • Motivating people.
    • Maslow suggests that people are motivate by satisfying their needs. Making sure that people’s social, esteem, and self-realization needs are satisfied is most important from a management point of view.
  • To satisfy social needs, you need to give people time to meet their co-workers and provide places for them to meet.
  • To satisfy esteem needs, you need to show people that they are valued by the organization. Public recognition of achievements is a simple yet effective way of doing this.
  • To satisfy self-realization needs, you need to give people responsibility for their work, assign them demanding tasks, and provide a training program where people can develop their skills. Training here is crucial.
  • Three personality types of professionals:
    • Task-oriented people, who are motivated by the work they do.
    • Self-oriented people, who are principally motivated by personal success and recognition.
    • Interaction-oriented people, who are motivated by the presence and actions of co-workers.

Teamwork
  • As a general rule, software engineering project groups should not have more than 10 members.
  • Smaller groups imply less communication issues.
  • Benefits of cohesive groups:
    • The group can establish its own quality standards. These standards will be more likely to be observed since they are established by consensus.
    • Individuals learn from and support each other. 
    • Knowledge is shared.
    • Refactoring and continual improvement is encouraged.
  • Three generic factors that affect teamwork:
    • The people in the group.
    • The group organization. 
    • Technical and managerial communications. 
  • Selecting group members:
    • A group that has complementary personalities may work better than a group that is selected solely on technical ability.
    • Interaction-oriented people are crucial to all groups due to the fact that they often improve cohesion.
  • Group organization:
    • Important organization questions for project managers:
      • Should the project manager be the technical leader of the group?
      • Who will be involved in making critical technical decisions, and how will these be made?
      • How will interaction with external stakeholders and senior company management be handled?
      • How can groups integrate people who are not colocated?
      • How can knowledge be shared across the group?
    • In informal groups (popular with XP), decisions such as making the schedule are left to the development team instead of management.
    • To make the most effective use of highly skilled programmers, some suggested that teams should be built around an individual, highly skilled chief programmer.
  • Group communications:
    • Group members must exchange information on the status of their work, the design decisions that have been made, and changes to previous design decisions.
    • Group communication quality is influenced by:
      • Group size. Keep groups on the small side.
      • Group structure. People in informally structured groups communicate more effectively than people in groups with a formal, hierarchical structure. In hierarchical groups, communication tends to flow up and down the hierarchy and people at the same level rarely talk to each other.
      • Group composition. Communication is usually better in mixed-sex groups.
      • The physical work environment.
      • The available communication channels.
    • Even though project managers work to tight deadlines, they must not be stingy on time spent in communication.
