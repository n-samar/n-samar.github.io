Encouraging Good Coding
  • In general mandating a strict set of technical standards from the management position isn’t a good approach to encouraging good coding.
  • Any technical standard is only as practiced as the developers buy into it
  • Have a respected architect define standards rather than the manager
  • Techniques for encouraging good coding:
    • Assign two people to every part of the project. Anything between pair programming to mentor-trainee pairs to buddy-system reviews.
    • Review every line of code.
    • Require code sign-offs. Senior technical personnel must sign code before it can be considered complete.
    • Route good code examples for review. Good management means communicating your objectives clearly. One way to communicate your objectives is to circulate good code to your programmers or post it for public display.
    • Emphasize that code listings are public assets. Code is not in property of a single developer.
    • Reward good code. Keep these considerations in mind as you develop your reinforcement system:
      • The reward should be something that the programmer wants.
      • Code that receives an award should be exceptionally good. If you give an award to a programmer everyone else knows does bad work, you look like Homer Simpson trying to run a nuclear reactor. If you are not technically skilled enough to make the good-code judgment, don’t!
    • One easy standard. An easy and effective technique for eliciting good work is to say “I must be able to read and understand any code written for the project.”

Configuration Management
  • Configuration management is the practice of identifying project artifacts and handling changes systematically so that a system can maintain its integrity over time. In one word — change management.
  • Without good change control rather than developing code you’re wasting your time thrashing.
  • The systemic problem with Software Configuration Management (SCM) is over control. (the surest way to stop car accidents is to prevent everyone from driving)

Requirements and Design Changes
  • If you implement each change as it occurs to you, you’ll soon find yourself walking on a software treadmill
  • Guidelines for controlling design changes:
    • Follow a systematic change-control procedure. This is very important to prevent the treadmill effect.
    • Handle change requests in groups. It’s tempting to implement easy changes as ideas arise. The problem with handling changes in this way is that good changes can get lost. Write diwb ask ideas and suggestions, no matter how easy they would be to implement, and save them until you have time to work on them. Then, viewing them as a group,choose the ones that will be the most beneficial.
    • Estimate the cost of each change. Estimate the time it would take to make the change.
    • Be wary of high change volumes. A high volume of change requests is a key warning sign that requirements, architecture, or top-level designs weren’t done well enough to support effective construction. This would be a good time to go back and reconsider requirements and architecture.
    • Establish a change-control board or its equivalent in a way that makes sense for your project.
    • Watch for bureaucracy, but don’t let the fear of bureaucracy preclude effective change control. Lack of disciplined change control is one of the biggest management problems facing the software industry today.

Software Code Changes
  • Version control is important
  • Benefits of version control:
    • You don’t step on each others toes while writing code
    • You can easily update your copies of all the project’s files to the current versions
    • You can backtrack to any version of any file that was ever checked into version control
    • You can get a list of the changes made to any version of any file.
    • You don’t have to worry about personal backups because the version control copy is a safety net.
    • If it is important to track compiler, linker and other support tool versions that are related to a specific version of the software, you can do that by adding these tools to version control.
  • Back up your work periodically. Take steps to safeguard you work.

Estimating a Construction Schedule
  • The average large software project is one year late and 100% over budget.
  • Estimation approaches:
    • Use estimating software
    • Use an algorithmic approach, such as Cocomo II, or Barry Bohm’s estimation model
    • Have outside estimation experts estimate the project
    • Have a walk-through meeting for estimates
    • Estimate pieces of the project, and then add the pieces together
    • Have people estimate their own tasks, and then add the task estimates together
    • Refer to experience on previous projects
    • Keep previous estimates and see how accurate they were. Use them to fudge new estimates.
  • Here’s a good approach to estimating a project:
    • Establish objectives. Why do you need an estimate? What are you estimating? How accurate does the estimate need to be to meet your objectives? Would an optimistic or a pessimistic estimate produce substantially different results?
    • Allow time for the estimate, and plan it.  Rushed estimates are inaccurate estimates.
    • Spell out software requirements. 
    • Estimate at a low level of detail.
    • Use several different estimation techniques, and compare the results. Study the different results from different approaches.
    • Reestimate periodically.

How to control resources to meet set schedule?
  • What to do if you’re behind?
    • Hope you will catch up. The rationalization typically goes like: “Requirements took a little longer than we expected, but now they’re solid, so we’re bound to save time later.” This is hardly ever the case. Delays and overruns typically increase towards the end of a project.
    • Expand the team. According to Fred Brooks, adding people to a late software project makes it later. Adding people to software projects in which the tasks can’t be divided and performed independently doesn’t help. If project is partitionable, adding people may work.
    • Reduce project scope. To make full use of this technique, partition project’s objectives into “must haves”, “nice to haves”, and “optionals”.

Measurement
  • Why measure processes:
    • For any project attribute, it’s possible to measure that attribute in a way that’s superior to not measuring it at all. To evaluate software-development methods, you must measure them.
  • Be aware of measurement side effects. Choose what you measure carefully. People tend to focus on work that’s measured and to ignore work that is not.
  • To argue against measurement is to argue that it’s better not to know what’s really happening on your project.

Treating Programmers as People
  • Top 20% of people produce about 50% of the output.
  • There are often order of magnitude differences between programmer effectiveness.
  • Good programmers seem to cluster together in teams (as do bad programmers).
  • Physical environment makes a big difference in productivity.
  • Most employees rate the quality of their workplace as unacceptable.
  • If you work for a technically aware manager, do whatever you can to keep your job. Otherwise manage your manager. The trick is to do it in a way that allows your manager to continue believing that you are the one being managed.
  • Some approaches to managing your manager:
    • Plant ideas for what you want to do, and then wait for your manager to have a brainstorm (your idea) about doing what you want to do.
    • Educate your manager about the right way to do things.
    • Focus on your manager’s interests, doing what he or she really wants you to do, and don’t distract your manager with unnecessary implementation details.
    • Refuse to do what your manager tells you, and insist on doing your job the right way.
    • Find another job.
