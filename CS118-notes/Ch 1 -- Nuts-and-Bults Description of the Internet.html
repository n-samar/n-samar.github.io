Chapter 1 — Nuts-and-Bolts Description of The Internet
  • End Systems are connected together by a network of communication links and packet switches. 
  • When one end system wishes to communicate to another, it segments the communication data into packets.
  • A Packet switch takes a packet arriving on one of its incoming communication links and forwards that packet on one of its outgoing communication links. These include routers and link-layer switches.
  • End systems access the Internet through Internet Service Providers (ISPs), which are in themselves a network of packet switches and communication links. Types of network access provided by ISPs include
    • Broadband access through cable modem or DSL
    • High-speed Local Area Network access
    • Wireless access
    • 56 kbps dial-up modem access
  • The user-level ISPs are connected together through upper-tier ISPs which consist of high-speed routers interconnected with high-speed fiber-optic links
  • All pieces of the Internet conform to protocols which control the sending and receiving of information on the Internet.
  • The Transmission Control Protocol (TCP) and the Internet Protocol (IP) are two of the most important protocols in the Internet.
  • The Internet Engineering Task Force (IETF) is responsible for specifying the standards of the Internet and describing them in documents called requests for comments (RFCs).

A Services Description
  • Description of the Internet as an infrastructure that provides services to applications including
    • electronic mail
    • web surfing
    • social networks
    • instant messaging
    • Voice-over-IP
    • Video streaming
    • Distributed games, etc.
  • These applications are called distributed applications
  • Importantly, although packet switches facilitate the exchange of data among end systems, they are not concerned with the application that is the source or sink of data.
  • The Internet provides The Internet API in order to allow many different platforms to communicate to each other and use the Internet.
  • The Network Edge is made up of all the end systems of the network
  • End systems are also called hosts and are divided into
    • Clients
    • Servers
  • Two most prevalent types of broadband residential access are
    • Digital Subscriber Line (DSL): Makes use of already existing telephone communication infrastructure. Usually obtained from the same local telephone company that provides its wired local phone access, making it the user’s ISP as well.
    • Cable: Makes use of existing television company’s infrastructure.
  • Residential telephone line carries both data and traditional telephone signals simultaneously, which are encoded at different frequencies (with 2 distinct frequency intervals for downstream and upstream internet).
  • The splitter splits incoming internet and phone data, directing the internet data to the DSL Modem on the customer side. On the telco side, the DSLAM divides telephone and internet signals and sends the Internet signals to the internet infrastructure.
  • One DSLAM can connect to hundreds or thousands of households.
  • The Central Office (CO) where the DSLAM is located must be within 5 to 10 miles of the attached household.
  • DSL provides 12/24 Mbps downstream and 1.8/2.5 Mbps upstream
  • Cable internet access uses television infrastructure, which has fast fiber-optic links to the central office, along with regular coaxial cable which leads to households. This sort of cable hybrid is called a hybrid fiber-coaxial access network.
  • Cable internet access requires special modems (cable modems). These connect to the home PC through an Ethernet port.
  • Cable provides downstream rates up to 42.8 Mbps and upstream of up to 30.7 Mbps.
  • Access for both DSL and Cable is considered asymmetric because the download and upload speeds are very disproportional. 
  • Fiber to the Home (FTTH) promises even greater speeds than DSL and Cable.
  • Two competing optical-distribution network architectures that perform splitting of signals from Central Office to Homes:
    • Active Optical Networks (AON)
    • Passive Optical Networks (PON)
  • The Passive Optical Network has one Optical Network Terminator (ONT) per household. The Optical Line Terminator (OLT) located in the central office connects a big set of homes and converts optical to electrical signals and vis versa. The Optical splitter replicates all packets received from the OLT.
  • FTTH could potentially offer speeds of up to gigabits per second.
  • Where DSL, cable, FTTH are not available, a satellite link can be used to connect households to speeds over 1 Mbps.

Access in the Enterprise: Ethernet and Wi-Fi
  • A Local Area Network (LAN) is used to connect an end system to the edge router.
  • Ethernet is a popular access technology in corporate.
  • With Ethernet access, users typically have 100 Mbps access to the Ethernet switch, whereas servers may have 1-10 Gbps access.
  • To provide wireless access, many Wireless LAN (WiFi) routers are provided which are themselves connected to the Ethernet switch. WiFi provides speeds of up to 54 Mbps.

Wide-Area Wireless Access: 3G and LTE
  •  Uses same infrastructure as cellular telephony to send/receive packets through a base station operated by the cellular network provider.
  • User must be within few tens of kilometers of the base station.

Physical Media
  • Guided media: The waves are guided along a solid medium, such as a fiber-optic cable, a twisted-pair copper wire, or a coaxial cable.
  • Unguided media: The waves propagate in the atmosphere and in outer space, such as wireless LAN or digital satellite channel.
  • In general, the cost of physical wires is far less than the cost of manual labor required to install it.
  • Twisted-Pair Copper Wire: least expensive and most commonly used guided transmission medium. Consists of two insulated copper wires, each about 1 mm thick, arranged in a regular spiral pattern. Each wire pair constitutes a communication link. 
  • Data rates for LANs using twisted pair today range from 10 Mbps to 10 Gbps. Dominant high-speed solution for LAN networking.
  • Coaxial cable: Like twisted pair, but the two conductors are concentric rather than parallel. This allows for higher data transmission rates. Common in cable television systems (and therefore, Cable Internet access). Can be used as guided shared medium, with multiple end systems sharing data with a set of others that are connected to the wire.
  • Fiber Optics: Thin, flexible medium that conducts pulses of light. 10-100 Gbps. Immune to electromagnetic interference. Hard to tap. Used for overseas links. Long-distance telephone networks in the US and elsewhere now use fiber optics exclusively.
  • Terrestrial Radio Channels: Carry signals in the electromagnetic spectrum. No physical wire required; can penetrate walls; long distance.
  • Satellite Radio Channels: Communication satellite links two or more Earth-based microwave transmitter/receivers, known as ground stations. The satellite receives transmissions on one frequency band, regenerates the signal using a repeater and transmits the signal on another frequency. Two types of satellites used:
    • Geostationary satellites: Stationary distance; substantial propagation delay (280 milliseconds); used in areas without DSL or Cable-based internet.
    • Low-earth orbiting (LEO) satellites: much closer; is not currently used for Internet.

Network Core
  • Store-and-Forward Transmission: Only let the packet switcher transmit data once full packet is buffered into it.
  • Queuing Delays and Packet Loss: for each attached link the packet switch has an output buffer which stores packets that the router is about to send into that link. If the link is busy with transporting some other packet, the packet switch must wait and this might cause buffer congestion. If the buffer gets completely full, packet loss may occur.
  • Forwarding tables and routing protocols: Each packet contains the destination’s IP address in its header. A forwarding table tells the packet switcher which addresses should be forwarded to which links.
  • Routing protocols are used to automatically set forwarding tables within routers.

Circuit Switching
  • Two approaches to moving data through network of links and switches:
    • Circuit switching: Resources needed along a path to provide for communication between the end systems are reserved for the duration of the communication session between the end systems. E.g. traditional telephone networks; ensures constant transmission rate
    • Packet switching
  • Frequency-division multiplexing (FDM): Frequency spectrum of a link is divided up among the connections established across the link.
  • Time-division multiplexing (TDM): Time is divided into frames of fixed duration
  • Packet switching may be less suitable for real-time services. However, packet switching offers better sharing of transmission capacity and it is simpler, more efficient and less costly to implement.
  • The expensive, overseas portions of calls are still managed through packet switching to lower costs.

A Network of Networks
  • Internet Exchange Point (IXP): meeting point where multiple ISPs peer together.
  • Multi-home: When an ISP decides to connect to multiple provider ISPs
  • PoP: Group of one or more routers at the same location in the provider’s network where customer ISPs can connect into the provider ISP.
  • Peer: when two ISPs from the same level directly connect their networks together so that all traffic between them passes directly through the peer rather than going through an upper-level intermediary.
  • Content Provider Networks: E.g. Google with 30 to 50 data centers distributed across the world. All Google’s data centers are interconnected in a private TCP/IP network, spanning the entire globe. Google also tries to bypass tier 1 ISPs by directly connecting to lower level (e.g. regional) ISPs thereby reducing payments to upper-level ISPs.

Delay, Loss, and Throughput in Packet-Switched Networks
  • Types of packet-switched network delays:
    • nodal processing delay: time required to read the packet’s header and determine where to direct it.
    • queuing delay: Time required for the packet to reach the top of the output buffer on the appropriate link.
    • transmission delay: The amount of time it takes for a packet to be transmitted over a link (limited by link throughput).
    • propagation delay: The time it takes for the signal to reach the next router from the link. Usually close to the speed of light.
  • Traffic intensity: determines if the router is able to process all incoming packets without them pilling up in the output queues (= <average rate of packet arrival> * <bits per packet> / <transmission rate>).
  • Packet loss: Occurs when the router's buffer becomes full and cannot hold additional packets.
  • The end-to-end delay will bi directly proportional to the number of routers between the end nodes.

  • Application-layer protocols (such as HTTP and SMTP) are almost always implemented in software in the end systems
  • The physical and data link layers are usually implemented in network interface cards associated with a given link.
  • The network layer is usually a mix of hardware and software. 
  • Application layer:
    • HTTP Protocol: provides for Web document request and transfer
    • SMTP Protocol: provides for the transfer of e-mail messages
    • FTP Protocol: provides file transfer between two end systems
    • Domain Name System (DNS): Provides translation from human-readable addresses (www.ietf.org to a 32-bit network address)
  • Transport layer:
    • TCP: provides connection-oriented service to applications; guaranteed delivery; flow control
    • UDP: provides connectionless service to its applications
  • Network layer:
    • Responsible for moving network-layer packets (datagrams) from one host to another.
    • IP Protocol: defines the fields in the datagram as well as how the end systems and routers act on these fields.
  • Link layer:
    • To move a packet from one node to the next node in the route, the network layer relies on the services of the link layer.
  • Physical layer:
    • moves the individual bits within a packet from one node to the next.
    • Link dependent and further depend on the actual transmission medium of the link.
    • E.g. Ethernet has many physical-layer protocols: one for twisted-pair copper wire, another for coaxial cable, another for fiber, and so on.

Networks Under Attack
  • Introducing malware into host via the Internet
    • Most malware is self-replicating: it seeks entry into further hosts.
    • Viruses are malware that require some form of user interaction to infect the user’s device (e.g. malicious executable code sent through spam emails).
    • Worms are malware that can enter a device without any explicit user interaction.
  • Attacks on servers and network infrastructure
    • Denial-of-service (DoS) attacks makes a network, host, or piece of infrastructure unusable by legitimate users. Most DoS are of three types:
      • Vulnerability attack. Involves sending a few well-crafted messages to a vulnerable application or OS running on a targeted host. The right sequence of packets would then cause the system or application to crash or halt.
      • Bandwidth flooding. The attacker sends a deluge of packets to the targeted host, clogging all the host’s buffers.
      • Connection flooding. Attacker establishes a large number of half-open or fully open TCP connections at the target host. Then the host stops accepting legitimate TCP connections.
    • Distributed Denial of Service Attacks (DDoS). Leveraging botnets with thousands of comprised hosts in order to make a DoS less noticeable.
  • Packet sniffers: Query all packets transmitted in physical vicinity through signal receivers.
    • Further, if an attacker gains access to an institution’s access router or access link to the internet it may sniff on all data that institution send/receives.
    • Mitigated through cryptographic mechanisms.
  • Miss-authentication(IP spoofing):
    • Mitigated through end-point authentication.
